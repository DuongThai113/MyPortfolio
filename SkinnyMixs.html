Skinny Mixes Demand Forecasting

Project Overview

This project develops a demand forecasting framework for Skinny Mixes using weekly order history. The analysis characterizes demand structure by examining long-run trend behavior, year-over-year seasonality, SKU-level heterogeneity, and external explanatory signals (e.g., macroeconomic variables and search interest). The objective is to produce decision-ready forecasts that support inventory planning, production scheduling, and operational risk management under demand uncertainty.

Data source: Internal order history provided by FST Logistics Inc. (as of February 2024); Tools: RStudio

Data Analysis

Main Questions

  • What underlying trends and seasonal patterns characterize demand for Skinny Mixes at both the aggregate and SKU levels?
  • How does demand variability differ across products, and what implications does this heterogeneity have for forecast accuracy and inventory planning?
  • To what extent can short-term demand be forecasted reliably given data volatility, SKU status changes, and external explanatory factors?

Results

Seasonality analysis:

  • Seasonality exists but is not stable year over year: Weekly demand shows recurring end-of-year peaks, but the magnitude and timing vary across 2022–2024. This instability limits the effectiveness of purely seasonal models when historical depth is limited.

Time-series decomposition:

  • Short-term volatility is driven by irregular spikes: Decomposition isolates a gradual upward trend, while the remainder component captures demand shocks and abrupt spikes. These irregular events increase forecast uncertainty and motivate frequent model refresh.

Comparative retailer context (Barnana vs. Skinny Mixes):

  • Forecast assumptions are not transferable across retailers: Barnana (a separate retail company, not affiliated with Skinny Mixes) exhibits sharper spikes and a less stable baseline. This comparison reinforces that model choice, preprocessing, and validation must be tailored to each business context.

Product-specific demand patterns:

  • Seasonal SKUs require event-aware planning: Gingerbread Latte demand concentrates in narrow seasonal windows, while Coconut shows a comparatively stable baseline with modest growth. These differences justify SKU-specific modeling and differentiated safety-stock policies.

External explanatory variables:

  • Useful for context, limited direct predictive lift: Macroeconomic variables and Google Trends provide qualitative signals, but measured effects appear delayed and modest. Additionally, these covariates must be forecasted for future periods, adding uncertainty and complexity to the pipeline.

Data quality implications (SKU status):

  • Annotation is a forecasting requirement: Stock-outs and discontinued products can create artificial zeros and structural breaks. Without explicit SKU status annotation, models may misinterpret operational constraints as true demand collapse, biasing forecasts and downstream planning decisions.

Forecast results and short-term projections:

  • Near-term growth is supported, but uncertainty increases over time: Forecasts indicate an upward trajectory (≈79k units in Week 9 to >107k units by Week 20). Widening uncertainty bands highlight increasing risk as the horizon extends—supporting frequent updates and continuous monitoring.

Analytical Implications

  • Data quality outweighs model complexity: Forecast performance is often constrained by annotation, outlier handling, and SKU lifecycle tracking more than by incremental model sophistication.
  • Segmentation is operationally necessary: SKU heterogeneity supports product-level or cluster-level forecasting to align replenishment with each SKU’s volatility and seasonality profile.
  • External signals should be complementary: Google Trends and macro indicators are best used as contextual inputs with careful lag specification and validation, rather than primary drivers.
  • Forecasting is a system, not a one-time model: Effective forecasting requires monitoring, periodic retraining, and governance to remain accurate under changing demand conditions.
